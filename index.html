Introduction

A treaty is a legally binding written agreement between sovereign states or international organizations, governed by international law.

Treaty 1 was the first Numbered Treaty signed between First Nations and the Government of Canada after Manitoba became a province. It marked the beginning of formal agreements between the Crown and Indigenous Nations in Western Canada.

Location

Treaty 1 was signed at Lower Fort Garry, a historic stone fort situated along the Red River in southern Manitoba. The treaty negotiations took place in front of the “Big House,” the residence of the Hudson’s Bay Company governor. In 1871, Lower Fort Garry was a key trading post and served as an important meeting place for both government officials and First Nations leaders, making it a central location for the historic agreement.

Significance

Treaty 1 was a pivotal agreement in Canadian history. It laid the groundwork for subsequent Numbered Treaties and provided a framework for cooperation, mutual respect, and the sharing of land and resources.

The treaty continues to hold considerable importance today. It affirms the rights of Indigenous Nations to their traditional territories in southern Manitoba and ensures that their cultural practices, such as hunting, fishing, and community traditions, are recognized and respected. Treaty 1 also serves as a reminder of the ongoing responsibilities of the government to honor its promises and work alongside Indigenous communities to support social, economic, and cultural well-being.
